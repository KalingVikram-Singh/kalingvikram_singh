\Abstract{The study includes a thorough theoretical analysis of the fundamental characteristics of superconductors, Josephson Junctions, and DC-SQUIDs. The three most significant phenomenological characteristics of superconductors: zero resistance, perfect diamagnetism, and macroscopic phase coherence have been introduced. Next, the fundamental characteristics of Josephson junctions RT, IV, and shapiro characteristics as well as their sensitivity to microwave signals have been covered. Two Josephson Junctions on a superconducting loop running parallel to one another make up a DC SQUID. The basic mechanism of SQUIDs have been explained to establish their flux-voltage conversion . The transition temperature of YBCO powder was determined using LC circuits, and experiments were carried out to determine various SQUID probe properties

\section{Introduction} % The \section*{} command stops section numbering
% \addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Introduction} % Adds this section to the table of contents
Kammerlingh Onnes and his group were first, in 1911, to report the existence of a superconductor, namely a  superconducting state of mercury at 4.2 Kelvin after liquifying Helium gas. Since then, different materials were tested for existence of the superconducting states and this lead to the theory of superconductivity.\newline

\noindent Supercondutors are materials with exceptional properties. Below a temperature, critical temperatures (T$_{c}$), superconductors lose all its electrical resistivity and become diamagnetic. These materials also show Meissner effect, the phenomena of expulsion of a magnetic field from a superconductor during its transition to the superconducting state when it is cooled below the critical temperature.
\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[scale=0.34]{type.png}
\caption{Type 1 and Type 2 superconductors [Hale].}
\label{fig:type}
\end{figure}


\noindent Superconductors are classified based on the strength of Meissner effect and critical temperature (T$_{c}$) as TYPE-1(low temperature superconductor) and TYPE-2 (high temperature superconductor). TYPE 1 supercondutors have a  T$_{c}$  $\leq$ 10K. They show perfect Meissner effect along perfect diamagnetism and very low critical field. The transition from non superconducting to superconducting of TYPE 1 superconductors is abrupt and show single critical fields. TYPE 2 semiconductors, on the other hand, have T$_{c}$ $\ge$ 10K. They partly show Meissner effect along with imperfect diamagnetism and comparatively higher critical fields. The transition of these superconductors has a mixed state that is between normal and the superconducting states, limited by two separate critical fields at the extremes of the mixed state as shown in \ref{fig:type}.\newline



\noindent In 1957,  Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) developed the theory of superconductivity to explain the physics behind superconductivity of TYPE 1 superconductors. The theory of superconductivity for TYPE 2 superconductors was given by Gilsberg and Landau in the same year.

\subsection{BCS Theory}
The BCS theory is based on the existence of Cooper pairs. Cooper pairs are a pair of electrons in a superconducting system which are attractively bounded to each other alongside having equal and opposite momenta and spins. Consider a pair of electrons just outside the fermi surface such that they have opposite momenta $\vec{K}$ and -$\vec{K}$. The hamiltonian is given by:

\begin{equation}
 H = 2\sum_{k}\epsilon_{k} + \sum_{k}V_{k}
\end{equation}

\noindent The states can be treated as superposition of multiple states, i.e, $\psi = \sum_{k}$g$_{k}\psi_{k}$. Using hamilton's equation, we have: 
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
 H\psi = E\psi \\
 g_{k}[E - 2\sum_{k}\epsilon_{k}] = \sum_{kk^{'}}v_{kk^{'}}g_{k^{'}}\\
 g_{k} = \frac{\sum_{kk^{'}}v_{kk^{'}}g_{k^{'}}}{E - 2\sum_{k}\epsilon_{k}}
 \end{split}
\end{equation}
Since g$_{k}$ are amplitudes, $\sum_{k}$g$_{k}$ = 1. The potential is given by:
\begin{equation}
    V_{kk^{'}} = 
    \begin{cases}
        V & \text{if } E_{0}^{f} < E < h\omega_{d}+ E_{f}^{0} \\
        0 & otherwise
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Thus, we get the integral:
\begin{equation}
\int_{E_{0}^{f}}^{E_{0}^{f} + h\omega_{d}}\frac{-VN(\epsilon)}{E-2\epsilon}d\epsilon
\end{equation}
Since the density of states is slowly varying function near the fermi surface, we can estimate it be N($\epsilon$) $\sim$ N(E$_{0}^{f}$). Thus, the eq (4) integrates to:
\begin{equation}
exp(\frac{2}{VN(E_{0}^{f})}) = 1-\frac{2\hbar\omega_{d}}{E - E_{0}^{f}}
\end{equation}
This leads to a pairing energy gap $\Delta$ given by $\Delta$ = E - 2E$_{0}^{f}$. For a weak coupling limit, $\Delta$ can be found through eq(5) as:
\begin{equation}
 \Delta = 2\hbar\omega_{d}exp(\frac{-2}{N(E_{0}^{f}V)})
\end{equation}
As long as the potential V is attractive and N(E$_{0}^{f}$V) is finite, we see formation of bound pairs of electrons. These bound pair of electrons are called Cooper pairs. After pairing up, these Cooper pairs behave as bosons and loose their fermionic behaviors. Formation of Cooper pairs have two important consequences: (a) The attraction between electrons is phonon mediated, and (b) The coulomb potential is screened in the material due to presence of positive ions in the metal [Kita].
\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[scale=0.19]{EP.jpg}
\caption{Electron - Phonon interaction.}
\label{fig:EP}
\end{figure}

\noindent For a metallic system with electron - phonon interaction (fig:\ref{fig:EP}) $>$ coulomb interaction, the formation of Cooper pairs is feasible. Classically, the displacement of the electrons from the mean position due to attractions towards positive ions lead to the electron - electron interaction mediated through a phonon. The pairing energy gap is experimentally verified and can be determined through methods like ultrasonic absorption below transition temperatures, IV characteristics  of electron tunneling, etc. Other mechanisms such as plasma or magnons can also lead to cooper pair formation, but phonon mechanism is the most common mechanism observed. A system with strong electron-phonon interaction is a poor conductor of electricity at room temperature, but is a good superconductor at absolute zero temperatures. It is a common trait that good conductors of electricity are worse superconductors compared to poor conductors of electricity.\newline

\noindent The BCS theory states that in a metallic system, all the electrons form cooper pairs. The corresponding energies and eigen states can be found using the following way:\newline

\noindent Let $\braket{k0|-k1}$ denote the cooper pair state. Let w$_{k}$ denote the probability that a system has an occupied cooper pair state. Also, let v$_{k}^{2}$ = w$_{k}$. The general eigen state is given by:
\begin{equation}
 \ket{\psi_{k}} = u_{k}\ket{0}_{k} + v_{k}\ket{1}_{k}
\end{equation}
where, $\ket{0}_{k}$ is unoccupied state and $\ket{1}_{k}$ is the occupied cooper pair state and $\ket{0}_{k}$ = $\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\ket{1}_{k}$ = $\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$. Also define the annihilation and creation operators $\sigma^{+}$ = ($\sigma^{1}$ + i$\sigma^{2}$)/2 and $\sigma^{-}$ = ($\sigma^{1}$ - i$\sigma^{2}$)/2, where $\sigma^{i}$ are pauli matrices. Hence, we get the following relations:
$$
  \sigma^{+}\ket{0}_{k} = \ket{1}_{k}, \sigma^{+}\ket{1}_{k} = 0 \\
$$
$$
  \sigma^{-}\ket{1}_{k} = \ket{0}_{k}, \sigma^{-}\ket{0}_{k} = 0
$$
Thus, the hamiltonian for electron - phonon interaction is given by:

\begin{equation}
 H_{PE} = -\frac{V_{0}}{2L^{3}}\sum_{kk'}(\sigma_{k'}^{+}\sigma_{k}^{-} + \sigma_{k}^{+}\sigma_{k'}^{-}) \\
 \end{equation}
\begin{equation}
 or \hspace{1mm}H_{PE} = -\frac{V_{0}}{L^{3}}\sum_{k}(\sigma_{k}^{+}\sigma_{k}^{-})
\end{equation}
Thus, the potential is restricted between $\pm$$\hbar\omega_{d}$ about the fermi energy. 
The BCS general eigen state is given by:
$$
\ket{\psi_{BCS}} = \prod_{k}\ket{\psi_{k}}
$$
The potential energy change is given by:
\begin{equation}
\bra{\psi_{BCS}}H_{PE}\ket{\psi_{BCS}} = \frac{-V_{0}}{L^{3}}\sum_{kk'}v_{k}u_{k'}u_{k}v_{k'} 
\end{equation}
Thus, the potential energy decreases due to coupling. On the other hand, the kinetic energy increases by:
\begin{equation}
 \bra{\psi_{BCS}}H_{KE}\ket{\psi_{BCS}} = \sum_{k}v^{2}_{k}\epsilon_{k}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_{k}$ = $\frac{\hbar^{2}k^{2}}{2m}$ - $E^{0}_{f}$. Lets define W$_{BCS}$ = $\sum_{k}v^{2}_{k}\epsilon_{k} - \frac{-V_{0}}{L^{3}}\sum_{kk'}v_{k}u_{k'}u_{k}v_{k'}$. The system with minimal W$_{BCS}$ is favourable since a system tries to minimize the total energy. We already know v$^{2}_{k}$ + u$^{2}_{k}$ = 1. Thus, minimizing W$_{BCS}$ with respect to $\frac{v_{}}{u_{}}$, we get:
$$
W^{0}_{BCS} = \sum_{k}\epsilon_{k}(1-\frac{\epsilon_{k}}{E_{k}}) - \frac{L^{3}}{V^{0}}\Delta^{2}
$$
where $\epsilon_{k}$ = $\frac{\hbar^{2}k^{2}}{2m}$ - $E^{0}_{f}$, $delta$ = $\frac{V_{0}}{L^{3}}\sum_{k'}sin\theta_{k'}cos\theta_{k'}$, E$_{k}$ = $\sqrt{\epsilon^{2} + \Delta^{2}}$, v$_{k}$ = cos$\theta_{k}$, and $u_{k} = sin\theta_{k}$. The necessary condition for minima is given by :
$$
sin(2\theta_{k}) = \frac{\Delta}{E_{k}}
$$
Using this, the energy gap can be simplified to give:
\begin{equation}
 \Delta E = -\frac{N(E^{0}_{f})\Delta^{2}}{2}
\end{equation}
The number of electrons in the system are n = $\frac{N(E^{0}_{f})\Delta}{2}$. Thus, the binding energy of each electron is given by -$\Delta$. Thus, we can say that all the electrons in the system form cooper pairs, and have a net energy gap of $\Delta$ for each pair. For weak coupling limit (N($E^{0}_{f}$))$<<$ 1, $\Delta$ can be simplified to give:
$$
\Delta \sim  2\hbar\omega_{d}exp(\frac{-2}{N(E_{0}^{f}V)})
$$
as shown in eq (6). Condensed matter physics' fundamental Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) theory accurately describes conventional superconductivity. But it has some serious drawbacks. High-temperature superconductors and materials with complicated underlying mechanisms are not explained by BCS theory, which is mostly applicable to low-temperature superconductors with weak electron-phonon interactions. It simplifies by ignoring effects of magnetic fields, electron-electron repulsion, and non-equilibrium processes. Furthermore, it falls short of explaining the atypical pairing with non-zero angular momentum and the high-temperature superconductivity isotope effect. Despite these flaws, other theories are being researched in order to account for a wider variety of superconducting materials and phenomena. BCS theory is nevertheless crucial for understanding conventional superconductors.

\subsection{Ginzberg - Landau Theory}
Lets define a order parameter, which is a measure of the degree of order across the boundaries in a phase transition system. This is zero outside the phase transition boundary while non zero within the limits of phase transition boundary.

\begin{equation}
    Order\hspace{1mm} parameter  
    \begin{cases}
        = 0 & \text{if } T > T_{c} \\
        \neq 0 & T < T_{c}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Let N$_{s}$ be the concentration of charge carriers in the superconducting state $\ket{\psi(\vec{r})}$. We know the following:
\begin{equation}
 N_{s}\propto \bra{\psi(\vec{r})}\mathbb{I}\ket{{\psi(\vec{r})}} \sim |\psi(r)|^{2}
\end{equation}
Expanding the free energy in terms of order parameter:
\begin{equation}
 f_{s} = f_{n} + \alpha(\psi^{2}) + \frac{\beta^{2}\psi^{4}}{2} + . . . - \int(\vec{M}.d\vec{B}) + \frac{(-i\hbar\nabla - qA)^{2}}{2m}
\end{equation}
where, $\alpha$, $\beta$ are constants that needs to be found out and $\psi$ = $\psi(\vec{r})$. For $\vec{B}$$\rightarrow$0, and keeping till biquadratic terms, we have:
\begin{equation}
 f_{n} - f_{s} = \alpha\psi^{2} + \frac{\beta\psi^{4}}{2}
\end{equation}
Minimizing the free energy with respect to the order parameter ($\psi$ or $\psi^{*}$), we get the following relations:

$$
(f_{s} - f_{n})_{min} = \frac{|\alpha|^{2}}{2\beta}
$$
$$
|\psi|^{2} = \frac{|\alpha|}{\beta}
$$
Since the critical thermodynamic field is given by:
\begin{equation}
(f_{s} - f_{n})_{min} = \frac{B^{2}_{c}}{2\mu_{0}} 
\end{equation}
Comparing eq 17 with $(f_{s} - f_{n})_{min}$, derived earlier, we get:
\begin{equation}
 B_{c} = \sqrt{\frac{\mu_{0}\alpha^{2}}{\beta}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{London penetration depth}
The magnetic field is a function of time when it is applied to a superconductor that is initially in zero field. The Maxwell equation states that the time-varying magnetic field generates the electric field. In a regular metal, this will cause eddy currents to form, but in a superconductor, the field will result in supercurrents, which are persistent currents. The magnetic field produced by the induced supercurrents will compete with the applied magnetic field. The flux is completely blocked off from the majority of the superconductor if the applied magnetic field is weak. Perfect diamagnetism is a common term used to describe this phenomena. In superconductors, the London penetration depth (usually denoted as $\lambda$  or $\lambda _{L}$) characterizes the distance to which a magnetic field penetrates into a superconductor and becomes equal to 
e$^{-1}$ times that of the magnetic field at the surface of the superconductor.

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[scale=0.35]{london.jpg}
\caption{London penetration depth.}
\label{fig:london}
\end{figure}


The force equation is given by:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}=m \frac{d \mathbf{v}_{\mathbf{s}}}{d t}=-e \mathbf{E} 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{v}_{\mathbf{s}}$ is the velocity of the superconducting carrier. The field-induced supercurrent density is given by
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{s}}=-e n_s \mathbf{v}_{\mathbf{s}},
\end{equation}
\noindent where $n_s$ is the local density of superconducting carriers. Substituting Eq. (20) into Eq. (19) gives
\begin{equation}
\frac{d \mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{s}}}{d t}=\frac{n_s e^2}{m} \mathbf{E} 
\end{equation}
which is known as the "first London equation". Taking the curl of both sides of Eq. (21) gives
\begin{equation}
\frac{m}{n_s e^2}\left(\nabla \times \frac{d \mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{s}}}{d t}\right)=\nabla \times \mathbf{E} 
\end{equation}
which can be rewritten using the Maxwell equation $\nabla \times \mathbf{E}=\frac{-1}{c} d \mathbf{B} / d t$ to give

\begin{equation}
\frac{m c}{n_s e^2}\left(\nabla \times \frac{d \mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{s}}}{d t}\right)+\frac{d \mathbf{B}}{d t}=0
\end{equation}
In order to obtain the Meissner effect (i.e. $\mathbf{B}=0$ in the bulk of the superconductor) the London brothers removed the time derivative in Eq. (23) and postulated the new equation:
\begin{equation}
\frac{m c}{n_s e^2}\left(\nabla \times \mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{s}}\right)+\mathbf{B}=0  
\end{equation}
Equation (24) is commonly referred to as the "second London equation". Since the supercurrent density is related to the field $\mathbf{B}$ by another Maxwell equation
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{J}_{\mathbf{s}}=\frac{c}{4 \pi}(\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) 
\end{equation}
Substitution of Eq. (25) into Eq. ( 24) gives
\begin{equation}
\lambda_L^2(\nabla \times \nabla \times \mathbf{B})+\mathbf{B}=0
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
 \frac{1}{\lambda_L^2}=\frac{4 \pi n_s e^2}{m c^2}
\end{equation}
The variable $\lambda_L$ is called the London penetration depth, as shown in fig \ref{fig:london}. The $\lambda_L^{-2}$ is proportional to the superfluid density $n_S$. of Eq. ( 26) is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{B}(x)=\mathbf{B}(0) \exp \left(-x / \lambda_L\right)
\end{equation}
Thus, both the magnetic field $B(x)$ and the supercurrent density $J_S(x)$ decay exponentially with distance inside the superconductor over the length scale $\lambda_L$.\newline

\noindent Thus from $\lambda_{L}$ from eq (27), we get:
\begin{equation}
 \lambda_{L} = \sqrt{\frac{m}{\mu_{0}q^{2}|\psi|^{2}}} = \sqrt{\frac{m\beta}{\mu_{0}q^{2}\alpha}} 
\end{equation}
\subsubsection{Spatial Variation of order parameter}
For a 1-D system with $\vec{B}$$\rightarrow$0,we can have:
\begin{equation}
 \frac{-\hbar^{2}}{2m}\frac{\partial^{2}\psi}{\partial x^{2}} + \alpha\psi + \frac{\beta\psi^{3}}{2} = 0
\end{equation}
Minimizing eq(30) and taking $\psi$ = $\psi_{\infty}$f, where $\psi$ is the state deep inside the superconductor and f is the order parameter. For a system with f = 1 + g, where
g $<<$1 (denotes the strength of the order), we have:
$$
f = 1+ g \sim \frac{\psi}{\psi_{\infty}}
$$
and the equation 30 reduces to:
\begin{equation}
 \frac{\partial^{2}g}{\partial x^{2}} - 2g\frac{2m\alpha}{\hbar^{2}} = 0
\end{equation}
The factor $\epsilon_{gl}$ = $\sqrt(\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2m\alpha})$ is called Gilsberg - Landau coherence length and g $\sim$ exp($\frac{-x\sqrt{2}}{\epsilon_{gl}}$). Thus, the order develops with the length. Hence, the order increases from 0 at the surface to $\psi_{\infty}$ deep within the superconductor over the length scale defined by $\epsilon_{gl}$.\newline

\noindent There are two competitive processes in the play, a higher london penetration depth increases the overall free energy of the system, while a higher coherence length leads to lowering of the overall free energy. Thus, one can classify TYPE 1 and TYPE 2 semiconductors based on the different length parameters. The G.L parameter is given by:
\begin{equation}
 \kappa = \frac{\lambda_{L}}{\epsilon_{gl}}
\end{equation}
Thus, we have the following criteria to determine the type of superconductor:

\begin{equation}
    Superconductor: 
    \begin{cases}
        = TYPE 1 & \text{if } \kappa < \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\
        = TYPE 2 & \text{if } \kappa > \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Josephson junctions and SQUID}
\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[scale=0.43]{josephson.png}
\caption{Josephson junction.}
\label{fig:josephson}
\end{figure}

Josephson's junction, as shown in \ref{fig:josephson},is a device that consists of two superconductors weakly coupled by a region which may be either nonsuperconducting or a weaker superconductor. In such devices, a coherent tunneling of cooper pairs occur, leading to a overall superconductor current, irrespective of the voltage between the two junctions. It is based on the quantum mechanical model of the superconducting state of the metal. The superconducting state $\bra{\psi}$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
 \ket{\psi} \sim \sqrt{\rho}exp(i\theta)
\end{equation}
where $\rho$ is the charge density and $\theta$ is the quantum mechanical phase. The josephson junction is the quantum analog of youngs double slit experiment. Similar to intensity of the young's double slit experiment being a funciton of phase difference $\theta$, the josephson current is a function of phase difference between the two quantum mechanical superconducting states, which is eventually a function of magnetic flux $\phi$. A remarkable use of josephson junctions is found in superconducting quantum interference device (SQUID) which is a device that measures very low fluctuations in the magnetic field, and works on the principle of josephson junctions.  
The equations are given by:
\begin{equation}
\frac{-i\hbar\partial{\phi_{1}}}{\partial{t}} = H_{1}\phi_{1} + T\phi_{2}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\frac{-i\hbar\partial{\phi_{2}}}{\partial{t}} = H_{2}\phi_{2} + T\phi_{1}
\end{equation}
Now, suppose, there is a voltage across the insulating region, thus we get:
$$
i\hbar \dot{n_{1}}sin\phi_{1} - (\hbar\dot{\phi_{1}} + \frac{qu}{2})n_{1}cos(\phi_{1}) = T\sqrt{n_{1}n_{2}}cos\phi_{2}
$$
$$
i\hbar \dot{n_{2}}sin\phi_{2} - (\hbar\dot{\phi_{2}} + \frac{qu}{2})n_{1}sin(\phi_{1}) = T\sqrt{n_{1}n_{2}}sin\phi_{2}
$$
Using these, we arrive at the equations:
\begin{equation}
 \dot{n_{1}} = \frac{2T}{\hbar}\sqrt{n_{1}n_{2}}sin(\phi_{2} - \phi_{1})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
 \dot{n_{2}} = -\frac{2T}{\hbar}\sqrt{n_{1}n_{2}}sin(\phi_{2} - \phi_{1})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
 \dot{\phi_{1}} = -\frac{iT}{\hbar}\sqrt{\frac{n_{2}}{n_{1}}}cos(\phi_{2} - \phi_{1}) - \frac{qu}{2\hbar}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
 \dot{\phi_{2}} = -\frac{iT}{\hbar}\sqrt{\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}}}cos(\phi_{2} - \phi_{1}) - \frac{qu}{2\hbar}
\end{equation}

\noindent If the superconductors S1 and S2 are identical, then we get the following:
\begin{equation}
 \dot{n_{1}} = -\dot{n_{2}} = \frac{2T}{\hbar}nsin(\phi_{2} - \phi_{1})
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
 \hbar(\phi_{2} - \phi_{1}) = qu
\end{equation}

Hence, even in the absence of the voltage, we see a voltage across the josephson junction. The quantum mechanical phase is given by:
\begin{equation}
\phi_{2} - \phi_{1} = \frac{qu}{\hbar} + \Delta\phi 
\end{equation}
The current across the junction is given by:
\begin{equation}
 I_{J} = \frac{2T}{\hbar}sin(\omega_{j}t + \Delta\phi)
\end{equation}
where, $\omega_{j} = \frac{2eu}{\hbar}$. With some further calculations, it can be found that:
\begin{equation}
 I_{J} = 2I_{0}sin(\delta)cos(\frac{eN\phi_{0}}{\hbar})
\end{equation}
where, $\phi_{0}$ = $\frac{\pi\hbar}{e}$. Hence, we see the josephson current maxima for integral multiple of flux quanta.
\subsection{Shapiro effect}
The current density is given by:
\begin{equation}
 J(t) = J_{0} sin(\delta(0) - \omega_{j}t)
\end{equation}
When the applied potential u is oscillatory (u = u$_{0}$ + u$_{a}$ cos($\omega_{a}$t)), we get the following equation:
\begin{equation}
 J(t) =  J_{0}\hspace{0.5mm}sin\hspace{0.5mm}(\delta(0) - \omega_{j}t - \frac{2eu_{a}}{\hbar\omega_{a}}sin(\omega_{a}t))
\end{equation}
This can be simplified to a Bessel's function given by:
\begin{equation}
 J(t) =  J_{0}\sum_{-\infty}^{\infty}B_{n}\frac{2eu_{a}}{\hbar\omega_{a}}sin(\delta(0) - (n\omega_{a} + \frac{2eu_{a}}{\hbar})t)
\end{equation}
The current density has a time independent DC part and a time dependent AC part. The AC part vanishes for n$\omega_{a}$ = -$\frac{2eu_{a}}{\hbar}$. Hence, when we see the IV characteristics, we observe step like graphs between current and potential, which as also termed as \textbf{Shapiro steps}. The steps are prominent in the microwave frequencies.
\subsection{Resistance vs Temperature characteristics}
When the T $>$ T$_{c}$, then the metal does not act as a super conductor and has a finite resistance. But, as the temperature is lowered, due to formation of cooper pairs, the whole system collapses to a single quantum state and leads to decrease in the resistance. Thus, we see a reduction in the resistance for T $<$ T$_{c}$
\subsection{SQUID chip}

A SQUID is a magnetometer that has TYPE 2 supercondutor thin film. It has multiple coils to account for modulation and external coupling on a long probe, which is covered by a magnetic shield. The chip contains 2 josephson junctions connected to one another across a voltage source. 
The SQUID offers Meissner effect, thus restricts any change in the magnetic flux. When there is a change in magnetic field, the SQUID negates the change in flux by applying current in the opposite direction. This current can be measured to find the change in magnetic flux. This further can be used as a DC voltmeter to measure the voltage change in a system. The itnernal circuitry can be seen in fig \ref{SQUD}.
\subsubsection{SQUID parameters}
\textbf{Loop inductance parameter:} The McCumber parameter measures the trade-off between the SQUID's noise performance and its sensitivity to magnetic flux fluctuations. Although a larger typically denotes a more sensitive SQUID, it can also result in greater noise. This variable aids in the design and optimisation of SQUIDs for certain uses, such as measuring magnetic fields. Mathematically, it is given by:
\begin{equation}
 \beta_{L} = \frac{2\pi L I_{c}}{\phi_{0}}
\end{equation}
Where, L is the inductance of the SQUID loop, $I_{c}$  is the critical current of the SQUID and $\phi_{0}$ is the magnetic flux quantum. Another way to relate loop inductance parameter is through the equation:
\begin{equation}
 \beta_{L} = \frac{4I_{c}R_{N}}{\pi\Delta V} - 1
\end{equation}
In order to take into account the fluctuations from temperature, the eq. (50) has some corrections as:
\begin{equation}
 \beta_{L} = \frac{4I_{c}R_{N}}{\pi\Delta V}(1- 3.57\frac{\sqrt{K_{b}TL}}{\phi_{0}}) - 1
\end{equation}

\noindent\textbf{McCumber parameter:}A shunt resistance is added to Josephson Junctions to prevent unwanted charging effects, however the capacitance can still ruin the arrangement by causing hysteresis in the current-voltage relation. We can bring a Junction/SQUID closer to perfection if we merely make sure that the shunt resistance is low enough to cancel out the resistive effects. McCumber parameter helps establishes this. Mathematically, it is given by:
\begin{equation}
\beta_{c} = \frac{2\pi R^{2}C}{\phi_{0}}
\end{equation}

Since two equivalent josephson junction are in play inside a SQUID chip, we get current and resistance contributions from both the junctions.  \hspace{2mm}I_{c} = 38.65 \mu A

$$V_{c} =  38.65 * (1.174 \pm 1.6806 * 10^{-4}) mV$$
$$= 45.37 \pm 6.495 * 10^{-3} mV $$

From fig \ref{fig:Vphi_char}, and the data acquired, we can find that $\Delta$ I = 20.2987$\mu$A corresponds to a single flux of quanta. This is an important parameter that would be used to find mutual inductance of the circuit. For the circuit, the mutual inductance is given by:

 M = \frac{\phi_{0}}{\Delta I}

$$

$$

$$
\Delta M = 0.7419 pF
$$
Thus, M = 50.9885 $\pm$ 0.7419 pF.

\subsection{Klystron wave generator}
The klystron's anode is a resonant cavity with perforated grids that allow accelerated electrons to pass through and continue travelling. However, a positive electrode does not go on to capture those electrons. Instead, they are forced to fall back into the cavity grids by a negatively polarised "reflector" as a result. The tube's operating goal is to have these electrons return to the cavity grids at precisely the right moment to strengthen the electric oscillatory field that is already present across these grids. Oscillations in the cavity are stimulated and sustained when this circumstance exists. If coaxial cable is utilised, microwave power is coupled out of the cavity by way of a loop; if a waveguide is used to transmit the power to the load, microwave power is simply coupled out of the cavity through an appropriate aperture. The spent electrons fall back to the positively biassed control grid where they are collected, adding to the control grid current after the electrons' kinetic energy has been given up to the oscillatory field of the cavity. When the tube is not oscillating, a significant amount of electrons are deflected by the reflector's retarding field with enough energy to travel through the cavity grids and subsequently be captured by the control grid. The oscillating electric field that appears across the cavity grids, nevertheless, absorbs most of the energy from falling electrons when oscillations are sustained in the cavity. Such electrons are then captured by the cavity grids, which serve as the equivalent of the diode's plate in this context.

\noindent The principle of electromagnetic wave resonance inside a closed cavity governs the operation of a frequency metre cavity, also referred to as a microwave resonator. It is frequently employed for accurate radio frequency and microwave measurements. The cavity is filled with a sample of the signal that has to be measured, as shown in fig \ref{meter}. By altering its dimensions, the cavity is brought into resonance at a particular frequency. The electromagnetic field inside the cavity is enhanced when the signal's frequency coincides with the cavity's resonant frequency, which results in a drop in output power. The position of the dip can be measured to identify the resonant frequency, which enables precise frequency monitoring of the input signal.
